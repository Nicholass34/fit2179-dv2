The Shifting
Drug Landscape

How Australia's relationship with smoking, vaping, alcohol, and illicit drugs has transformed across three decades and what the numbers reveal about where we're heading.

By Nicholas · Monash University · May 2026 · Sources: AIHW NDSHS · ABS Causes of Death · AIHW AODTS

01

The Big Picture

Four substances. Four very different trajectories. Over three decades, Australia has quietly transformed its drug habits but not always in the direction you'd expect.

For most of the twentieth century, smoking was the defining health crisis of Australian life. A quarter of all adults lit up daily. Today, that number has collapsed to under one in ten the steepest fall of any substance ever tracked.

But while smoking has retreated, other habits have moved in. Vaping, virtually non-existent a decade ago, now reaches one in five young adults. Risky drinking has barely shifted. And the use of illicit drugs is creeping upward especially among groups you might not predict.

The smoking decline took thirty years and was driven by relentless policy: graphic warnings, plain packaging, advertising bans, tax hikes. But the next substance to surge vaping has barely been around long enough for the policy response to catch up.

Meanwhile, alcohol Australia's most culturally embedded substance has refused to budge. Despite decades of public health campaigns, nearly one in three Australians still drinks at levels that exceed national guidelines.

"Four substances, four trajectories. The story of Australian drug use isn't one story it's four happening at once."
02

Where the Risk Lives

Drug and alcohol patterns aren't uniform across Australia. Some states stand apart combining high rates of smoking, drinking, or drug-induced deaths in ways that reflect deeper geographic and demographic divides.

Australia's drug map is not what most people imagine. The Northern Territory and Tasmania emerge as twin hotspots combining the country's highest rates of both daily smoking and risky drinking. But high use doesn't always translate to high mortality. When we shift the lens to drug-induced deaths, a different geography emerges, with Western Australia leading.

03

Different Drugs, Different People

Every substance has its own demographic fingerprint. Some have shifted dramatically across generations. Others have stayed remarkably stable. The age dimension reveals what aggregate numbers hide.

No single chart captures the diversity of substance use across age groups quite like a heatmap. Read across each row to see how an age group splits its preferences. Read down each column to see how a substance is distributed across generations.

Cannabis dominates the illicit drug landscape. But the rest of the picture shows something surprising: pharmaceutical misuse is now mainstream, and cocaine has overtaken the substances we typically associate with addiction.

The most striking demographic story is one of generational divergence. Young Australians have largely abandoned smoking yet their parents and grandparents have moved toward illicit drugs, with use among 50+ Australians more than doubling since 2001.

04

The Cost in Lives and Care

Behind every percentage point is a system of harm emergency rooms, treatment programs, families, funerals. The mortality data and treatment data together reveal which substances do the most damage.

Australia has lived through two drug-death epidemics: the heroin crisis at the turn of the millennium, and the slower-burning opioid era that followed. Combining drug-induced and alcohol-induced deaths shows the total burden has stayed remarkably stable around 13 per 100,000. But the composition is shifting beneath the surface.

The trajectory

The composition

Within drug deaths, the ranking is shifting too. Opioids still dominate. But stimulants and cocaine have moved up the rankings in the past five years, while traditional pharmaceutical overdoses have begun to fall.

Treatment data reveals what survey data cannot. The substances people seek help for alcohol, amphetamines, cannabis paint a different picture than what they admit to using. Australia's treatment system is overwhelmingly an alcohol treatment system.

05

What Australians Want

Drug policy is fiercely contested. But when you ask Australians directly, a surprisingly clear picture emerges one that favours pragmatism over punishment, and treatment over legalisation.

The Australian public, when surveyed, holds two parallel views. The first: drug use should be treated as a health issue. The second: drugs themselves should remain illegal. Both views are held strongly, often by the same people.

Nine of the top ten supported policies are harm-reduction measures needle exchanges, take-home naloxone, pill testing. Legalisation, by contrast, remains a minority position even for cannabis, which has overwhelmingly normalised in everyday life.

06

Three Decades, One Conclusion

Australia's relationship with drugs and alcohol is not improving so much as it is rearranging. Smoking is collapsing. Vaping is exploding. Drinking is stuck. Illicit drug use is aging. The total burden of substance harm has plateaued, but the composition is restless and shifting.

What the data makes unmistakable is that there is no single Australian drug story. There are four, or six, or fifteen and they each demand a different response. The policy interventions that crushed smoking will not work for vaping. The treatment system built around alcohol must be retooled for stimulants. The age groups we worry about today are not the ones we need to worry about tomorrow.

The numbers don't tell us what to do. But they tell us where to look.
